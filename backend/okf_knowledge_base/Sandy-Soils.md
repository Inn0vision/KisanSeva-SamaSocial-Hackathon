# Sandy Soils
Sandy soils are characterized by a high proportion of sand particles, making them highly permeable due to larger sand grains and pore spaces. Consequently, they have low water retention capacity and offer limited protection to [[Soil Organic Matter (SOM)]] compared to soils with higher proportions of silts and clays. The text notes that sandy soils are generally the least productive due to their inability to retain moisture and nutrients effectively through chemical attraction. However, they can be managed productively in hot, dry climates with adequate water, organic materials, and fertilizers.

# Sandy Soils
Sandy soils are inherently permeable and, in hot, dry areas, experience high evaporation rates and rapid organic matter breakdown. For these reasons, the accumulation rate of organic matter is expected to be lower in sandy soils, especially in drylands. Crop management systems that reduce soil disturbance and promote high accumulation of organic matter are recommended for these soils.

# Sandy Soils
Soils composed primarily of sand particles, often prone to erosion and degradation. The text mentions that Zaï Planting pits and Stone Bunds were adopted to "reduce pressure on already-cultivated sandy soils" in drylands, indicating their susceptibility to degradation.
