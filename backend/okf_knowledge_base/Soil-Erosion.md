# Soil Erosion
Soil erosion is identified as a major concern and a form of land degradation. Agroforestry systems contribute to preventing soil erosion, particularly on slopes, and studies have shown they can reduce erosion significantly. The combination of drought and overgrazing is also noted to destroy vegetal cover and increase soil erosion. Increasing [[Soil Organic Matter]] and combining it with soil cover (as in conservation agriculture) helps reduce soil erosion.

# Soil Erosion
Soil erosion is the process by which soil particles are detached and transported by natural forces like water and wind. The text highlights that more intense rainfall and storms, a consequence of climate change, increase the risk of soil erosion by water (through rain splash, accelerated runoff) and wind (through strong winds). [[Degraded Soils]] with losses of [[Soil Organic Matter (SOM)]] are particularly vulnerable to increased rates of soil erosion and landslides. Controlling soil erosion is a key principle for climate change adaptation and mitigation.

# Soil Erosion
Soil erosion is the displacement of the upper layer of soil, a form of soil degradation. Curbing soil erosion is identified as an important method to reduce fertilizer losses, thereby protecting the environment and cutting costs for farmers.

# Soil Erosion
The washing or blowing away of the top layer of soil. It is identified as a form of land degradation and a driver of ecosystem stress that contributes to underlying disaster risk. Addressing such drivers is a goal of both Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR) and Climate Change Adaptation (CCA).
